Acquiring Deemed Conveyance

A simplified guide to legally securing the title of the land and building in the name of the Co-operative Housing Society.

1

Preparation and Notice to Builder

The society takes the first legal step by collating necessary property documents and serving a formal legal notice to the original builder/landowner demanding the conveyance of the land.

  • Document Collection: Gather the 7/12 Extract, Property Card, Society Registration Certificate, and approved building plans.
  • Legal Notice: Send a formal legal notice via an advocate to the Promoter/Builder granting them a statutory period (usually 30 days) to execute regular conveyance.
  • Resolution: Pass a resolution in the General Body Meeting to initiate Deemed Conveyance proceedings if the builder ignores the notice.
2

Filing Application to Competent Authority

Upon the builder's failure to comply, the society formally files an application for Deemed Conveyance with the District Deputy Registrar (DDR) / Competent Authority.

  • Form VII Application: Submit the application in the prescribed Form VII along with the required court fee stamp.
  • Indexing: Prepare a precise index of all attached documents (agreements, OC, CC, property cards, legal notice copies).
  • Architect Certificate: Submit a certificate from an architect detailing the area to be conveyed and FSI consumed/retained.
3

Scrutiny and Issue of Notice

The Competent Authority scrutinizes the submitted application. If found in order, official notices are issued to the builder and landowners.

  • Verification: The DDR's office verifies that every required annexure is attached and properly stamped.
  • Summons to Builder: Official summons are dispatched by the Competent Authority to the builder/landowners to appear for a hearing.
  • Public Notice: A notice is published in at least two local newspapers (one English, one vernacular) calling for objections from the public.
4

Hearings and Evidence Verification

Formal quasi-judicial hearings take place. The society's legal representative presents evidence, while the builder gets a chance to respond.

  • Argument Representation: The society’s advocate proves that statutory terms of conveyance have not been met.
  • Developer's Reply: The builder/landowner submits their written say and objections, if any, justifying the delay.
  • Exparte Order: If the builder willfully ignores the summons, the authority can proceed to hear the matter *exparte* (in their absence).
5

Passing of Deemed Conveyance Order

After hearing both parties, the Competent Authority passes a final judgment granting Deemed Conveyance and issues a valid Certificate.

  • Reasoned Order: A detailed written order is issued by the DDR justifying the grant of deemed conveyance to the society.
  • Certificate Issuance: A formal "Deemed Conveyance Certificate" is granted, conferring title rights to the society.
  • Draft Approval: The DDR officially approves the Draft Conveyance Deed submitted by the society.
6

Adjudication, Registration & Mutation

The final step: resolving stamp duty, registering the deed at the Sub-Registrar's office, and officially entering the society's name in revenue records.

  • Stamp Duty (Adjudication): The document is submitted to the Collector of Stamps. If members paid stamp duty on individual agreements, only a nominal fee is required.
  • Registration: The executed Deemed Conveyance Deed is registered with the Sub-Registrar of Assurances.
  • Mutation Entries: Applications are made to the City Survey Office (for Property Card) and Talathi (for 7/12 extract) to formally reflect the society as the absolute owner.
